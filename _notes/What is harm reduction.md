# What is harm reduction?

_Broadly speaking:_ Harm reduction describes a set of ideas, strategies, practices and policies which seek to reduce the negative consequences associated with drug use.

There is no single agreed-upon definition, and it means different things to different people. While harm reduction has been coopted by formations like nonprofits and municipal public health departments, I believe there are some guidelines by which we can delineate what I'll call, for lack of a better term, *true* harm reduction.

1. Since many of the negative consequences of drug use are tied to drug prohibition under capitalism, ==harm reduction must be **[anti-prohibitionist](The%20iron%20law%20of%20prohibition.md)** and **anti-capitalist**, adhering to **abolitionist** values==.
2. Recognizing the disproportionate effect that decades of bad drug policy have had on communities of color, ==harm reduction must also be **anti-racist**==.
3. ==Harm reduction must respect bodily autonomy and consent==, including the right for people to consume drugs in as safe and predictable of a manner as possible. In practice, this means fighting for a supply of drugs that is free from deadly contaminants. [Such a supply can only exist once these drugs cease to be criminalized.](The%20iron%20law%20of%20prohibition.md)
4. People deserve access to the tools that will best suit their needs according to their present relationship with drugs. 
5. Any organization "doing" harm reduction work must reflect the population it seeks to serve. ==The meaningful participation of people who use drugs and/or sex workers is not only important for this work, it is vital to its effectiveness.==

Patterns of chaotic or maladaptive drug use can be disabling, preventing people from selling their labor (the criteria for disability under capitalism) and ultimately diminishing their quality of life. For this reason, harm reduction must be in solidarity with and learn from movements for **disability justice.**
